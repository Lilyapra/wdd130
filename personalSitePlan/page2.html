The History of the Internet Timeline

Over the next few decades, this small network grew, evolving into a worldwide system that would forever change how we interact, learn, and work. From the invention of packet-switching to the creation of the World Wide Web, the internet’s story is one of technological innovation and collaboration. This timeline explores the key milestones that turned a simple idea into one of humanity’s most transformative achievements.

• 1957 - Sputnik and ARPA: The Soviet Union’s launch of Sputnik prompts the U.S. to form the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) to fund technology research.

• 1961 - Packet Switching Concept: MIT researcher Leonard Kleinrock introduces the idea of packet-switching, the basis for data transfer on networks.

• 1966 - ARPANET Development Begins: ARPA, with various research universities, initiates a plan to develop ARPANET, a network for military communication.

• 1969 - First ARPANET Message: On October 29, UCLA and Stanford Research Institute transmit the first "login" message over ARPANET, but the system crashes after “LO.”

• 1969-1970 - ARPANET Expansion: Four initial nodes (UCLA, SRI, UCSB, and University of Utah) establish communication, showing the network's potential.

• 1971 - Email is Invented: Ray Tomlinson, a researcher, invents email, using the "@" symbol for the first time in network addresses.

• 1973 - First International Connection: ARPANET expands overseas, linking to a node in Norway and marking the start of international networking.

• 1974 - Transmission Control Protocol (TCP): Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn develop TCP, allowing reliable data transmission, laying the groundwork for modern internet protocols.

• 1976 - Ethernet is Born: Robert Metcalfe invents Ethernet, creating a local network solution that becomes the backbone of LAN technology.

• 1983 - TCP/IP Standardized: ARPANET switches to TCP/IP, becoming the standard protocol suite, officially connecting separate networks into a cohesive “internet.”

• 1984 - Domain Name System (DNS): DNS is created, organizing the internet into domains, making web addresses easier to remember.

• 1989 - Proposal for the World Wide Web: British scientist Tim Berners-Lee proposes the concept of a "web" that uses hypertext to connect documents.
